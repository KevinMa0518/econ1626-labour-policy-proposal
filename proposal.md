# AI Labour Market Adjustment and Shared Productivity Package

## Executive Summary
Artificial intelligence (AI), especially generative AI, is changing work through task automation, task augmentation and new demand for AI-complementary skills. The main policy problem is not immediate mass unemployment, but uneven adjustment: some workers and firms will capture productivity gains, while clerical, administrative and entry-level professional workers may face task displacement, wage pressure or weaker career pathways. I recommend a three-part package: an AI Skills Guarantee, an AI Transition Service, and Responsible AI at Work rules linked to SME adoption support. This balances productivity and fairness. It supports workers before displacement happens, helps affected workers move into growing roles, and reduces harms from opaque workplace AI. DEWR should implement the package over three years with Jobs and Skills Australia, states, unions, employers and training providers.
## Problem Definition and Context
AI should be treated as a general-purpose technology because it can be used across many sectors, rather than being limited to one industry. Its labour-market impact works through tasks. Some tasks are substituted by software, some are made faster by AI assistance, and some new tasks are created around data, AI governance, human review and system integration. This means “AI exposure” is not the same as job loss. However, exposure still matters because it changes labour demand, bargaining power and the skills workers need.

International evidence suggests the shock is broad. The IMF estimates that about 60% of jobs in advanced economies may be affected by AI, with some roles benefiting from productivity gains and others facing lower labour demand (IMF 2024). The ILO also finds that generative AI is more likely to transform job quality and task content than fully replace entire occupations, with clerical work particularly exposed (ILO 2025). For Australia, the challenge is urgent because AI adoption is occurring while productivity growth has been weak and employers already report skills shortages. The Productivity Commission argues that AI could produce significant productivity gains, but only if regulation, data access and workforce capability settings support adoption (Productivity Commission 2025).

## Analysis of Labour Market Impacts
AI’s first impact is productivity. In customer support, Brynjolfsson, Li and Raymond (2025) found that a generative AI assistant increased productivity on average, especially for less experienced workers. This shows AI can reduce skill gaps by spreading the practices of high performers. At the economy level, higher productivity can lift real wages, but only if the gains are shared through competitive markets, worker capability and fair bargaining.

The second impact is task change. In Australia, Future Skills Organisation (2023) finds that higher-skill occupations, especially those relying on cognitive skills, are more exposed to generative AI. This differs from earlier automation waves that mainly affected routine physical or production work. AI can now affect writing, coding, analysis, customer service, accounting support and administration. Jobs and Skills Australia (2024) also shows that AI-skill demand is concentrated in ICT, data and professional services, but it is spreading into non-ICT roles such as project work and sales. This implies that AI literacy should not be limited to software developers.

The third impact is wages and equity. Workers who can use AI may become more productive and earn higher wages, while workers whose tasks are automated may face weaker demand. The distributional risk is strongest for clerical and entry-level roles because these jobs often provide first career steps for young people, migrants and women. If junior tasks are automated without redesigning training pathways, firms may still need senior workers but hire fewer beginners. This would damage long-run skill formation.

The fourth impact is industrial structure. AI may increase market concentration if large firms have better access to data, computing power and foundation models. This matters for labour markets because concentrated firms may capture productivity gains as profits instead of wages, and smaller firms may fall behind. Therefore, labour-market policy should connect with competition, data and responsible AI policy.

## Policy Options and Evaluation
| Option | Efficiency | Equity | Feasibility | Main weakness |
|---|---|---|---|---|
| 1. AI Skills Guarantee | High: improves human-AI complementarity and supports adoption | Medium-high: can target exposed workers and SMEs | High: can build on VET, universities and employer training | Training alone may not help displaced workers quickly |
| 2. AI Transition Service | Medium: reduces frictional unemployment and speeds reallocation | High: supports workers most exposed to displacement | Medium: requires data sharing and service coordination | More costly and may be used too late |
| 3. Responsible AI at Work + SME adoption support | High: builds trust and encourages safe adoption | High: gives workers voice and limits unfair surveillance or bias | Medium-high: aligns with existing AI safety guidance | Compliance burden if rules are too complex |

Option 1 would fund short, stackable AI micro-credentials in AI literacy, data handling, prompt use, cyber safety and occupation-specific AI tools. It should prioritise clerical workers, early-career professionals, regional workers and SMEs. This option is efficient because it raises complementarity between workers and AI, but it is insufficient if workers have already lost jobs.

Option 2 would create an AI Transition Service using Jobs and Skills Australia data to identify exposed occupations, then provide career coaching, training credits and temporary wage insurance for workers moving into shortage or growth areas. This is strongest on equity because it directly supports adjustment, but it needs careful targeting to avoid becoming a broad subsidy.

Option 3 would require large employers and high-risk AI users to consult workers, complete AI impact assessments, provide human review for consequential workplace decisions, and disclose when AI is used in monitoring, rostering or performance management. To avoid slowing adoption, SMEs should receive AI adoption vouchers and simple templates based on the Australian Voluntary AI Safety Standard (DISR 2024).
